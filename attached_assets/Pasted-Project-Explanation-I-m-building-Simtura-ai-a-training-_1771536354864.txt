Project Explanation
I'm building Simtura.ai, a training platform for- at this point EMS personnel- which uses AI to create realistic interactive video scenarios that help to train upcoming EMTs, paramedics, and so on. Through using realistic-looking simulations that fluidly evolve like real scenarios, the immersion of students into scenario training becomes more effective than ever before. Such a program helps to bridge the wide gap between classroom teaching and real world experiences, allowing students to sharpen critical decision making far beyond their first ever call. 
__________________________________________

Problem Solved
In any discipline, practice makes perfect. Yet in fields such as emergency medical services or law enforcement, opportunities for real-world, on-the-job training are often limited by logistics, safety concerns, and availability. You can’t simply ride along in an ambulance or shadow every critical call. Simtura.ai overcomes this barrier by generating seamless, high-fidelity scenarios through advanced AI. These simulations allow students to hone their decision-making, communication, and technical skills in a safe yet authentic environment. By the time they face their first real-world emergency, the scene will feel less like uncharted territory—and more like second nature.

Background
This summer, I participated and completed an EMT course, finally becoming certified on the first of August. Each week, I learned through a textbook and completed online quizzes about the knowledge I’d gained from my readings. Then, during my 10 hour classes on Saturdays I would go to a classroom and practice hands on skills. I found that I learned much more when I was using the knowledge I’d learned in what was supposed to be a practical application. But, I found that my time doing so was limited- I could only use the dummies once a week and I had to wait for the other 30 kids in my class to have their turn before I could. Further, it didn’t feel real- they were only dummies after all. Among these thoughts came forth the idea Simtura.ai represents.

Scenario #3 – Bag Valve Mask (Precert)
72-year-old male, unresponsive in bed
Initial Findings: RR 4, weak pulse 52, SpO₂ 70%, HR 52, BP 92/58, cyanotic, cool/clammy, groans to pain only. Snoring respirations.
Progression:
Without intervention: Pulse weakens further, SpO₂ drops to 64%, respirations stop.
With BVM + O₂ (15 L/min): Chest rise improves, SpO₂ increases to 86% within 2 minutes. HR rises to 66, skin color improves slightly. Patient begins to move arms but remains non-verbal.


“Equip BSI.” (gloves, mask, eye protection, etc.)
“Is my scene safe?” (look around, no hazards noted).
“I am going to call ALS.”
“There is 1 patient.”
“The nature of illness is respiratory failure.”
“Not going to consider spinal precautions, no indication.”
“The patient is unresponsive, groans to painful stimuli only.”
“Airway is not patent — patient has snoring respirations, so I will open the airway with a head-tilt chin-lift and insert an OPA.”
“Breathing is agonal and inadequate, respiratory rate 4 with cyanosis present. I will immediately begin ventilations with a BVM.”
“Attach BVM to high-flow oxygen at 15 liters per minute and assist ventilations at a rate of 10–12 breaths per minute, making sure I see chest rise.”
“Circulation: Patient has weak radial pulse at 52, skin is cool and clammy, SpO₂ is 70%. This is consistent with poor perfusion. Blood pressure is 92/58.”
“This is a high-priority patient.”
“Continue ventilations — patient’s oxygen saturation improves to 86%, pulse rises to 66, skin color improves slightly. The patient begins moving arms but remains non-verbal.”
“Let’s load the patient onto the stretcher and begin rapid transport to the ambulance.”
“I will gather SAMPLE/OPQRST history en route since patient is unstable and should not be delayed on scene.”
OPQRST SAMPLE
“I would like to recheck the patient’s vitals: BP 92/58, HR 66, RR assisted at 12/min, SpO₂ 86%, EtCO₂ pending.”
“Continue ventilations en route, reassess every 5 minutes due to critical condition.”
“We have arrived at the hospital. I will transfer care of my patient and provide a full verbal report.”


Scenario #4 – Trauma (Precert)
22-year-old male, thigh laceration from glass
Initial Findings: Spurting arterial bleed, HR 132, BP 90/60, SpO₂ 95%, pale/anxious. Cap refill >3 sec.
Progression:
Without bleeding control: BP drops to 76/40, HR 160, patient becomes lethargic, skin ashen.
With tourniquet applied: Bleeding stops. HR stabilizes around 118, BP rises to 100/64. Patient becomes more coherent but remains pale and weak.

“Equip BSI.” (gloves, mask, eye protection).
“Is my scene safe?” (look for broken glass, environmental hazards).
“I am going to call ALS.”
“There is 1 patient.”
“The mechanism of injury is penetrating trauma with severe external hemorrhage.”
“No indication for spinal precautions at this time.”
“The patient is alert but anxious, pale, with spurting arterial bleeding from the thigh.”
“First, I will apply direct pressure to the wound with a sterile dressing.”
“Bleeding is uncontrolled with direct pressure — I will immediately apply a tourniquet high and proximal on the thigh until bleeding stops.”
“Airway is patent. Patient is talking to me.”
“Breathing is adequate, respiratory rate not distressed, SpO₂ is 95%.”
“Circulation: Spurting arterial bleed controlled with tourniquet, radial pulse is rapid and weak, HR 132, BP 90/60, cap refill >3 seconds, skin is pale and cool.”
“This is a high-priority patient due to shock from hemorrhage.”
“Expose and assess for additional injuries, manage life threats.”
“Reassess bleeding — bleeding controlled with tourniquet. HR stabilizes to 118, BP rises to 100/64. Patient more coherent but remains pale and weak.”
“Let’s load the patient onto the stretcher for rapid transport.”
“I will treat for shock: keep the patient warm with blankets, elevate legs if no contraindication, provide high-flow oxygen via NRB at 15 L/min.”
“I will gather SAMPLE/OPQRST history en route since the patient is unstable and should not be delayed on scene.”
“I would like to recheck the patient’s vitals: HR 118, BP 100/64, RR 20, SpO₂ 95%.”
“Continue to reassess bleeding control, mental status, and perfusion every 5 minutes.”
“We have arrived at the hospital. I will transfer care of my patient and give a full verbal report.”


Scenario #5 – Combative Overdose (NewCert)
30-year-old female, suspected heroin use
Initial Findings: Unresponsive, agonal respirations 6/min, SpO₂ 72%, HR 48, BP 88/54, pupils pinpoint, skin cool.
Progression:
With BVM + naloxone (IN 4 mg): Within 2 minutes, respirations increase to 14/min, HR rises to 78, BP 108/70, SpO₂ 94%. Patient regains consciousness, becomes combative, pushes rescuers away, shouting she’s “fine.”
If no intervention: Respirations cease, HR bradycardic to 30, cardiac arrest within 5 minutes.

Scenario #6 – Motor Vehicle Collision, Multi-Patient (NewCert)
Driver #1: Alert, bleeding scalp wound. HR 110, BP 118/74, SpO₂ 98%. Becomes more agitated, vomiting blood → airway risk. 
Passenger #1: Initially unresponsive, shallow breaths at 8/min, SpO₂ 82%, HR 52, BP 88/56. With BVM: RR rises to 12 assisted, SpO₂ improves to 91%, HR increases to 72. Without airway control, descends into arrest.
Driver #2: Neck pain, holding still. HR 98, BP 130/78, SpO₂ 99%. Stable if immobilized.

Scenario #7 – Elderly Fall, Possible Head Injury (NewCert)
82-year-old female, fall in nursing home, on warfarin
Initial Findings: Alert but confused. Forehead hematoma. HR 88, BP 158/86, RR 20, SpO₂ 95%. GCS 14.
Progression: Over 10 minutes, speech slurs, right-sided arm weakness. HR 92, BP rises to 174/90, SpO₂ 94%.
Response: O₂ improves SpO₂ to 98%, but neuro deficits worsen → GCS drops to 12 en route.

Scenario #8 – Tension Pneumothorax (Recert)
29-year-old male, chest trauma from steel beam
Initial Findings: Severe distress, RR 36, SpO₂ 82%, HR 144, BP 86/60, cool/clammy, JVD, tracheal deviation left, absent L breath sounds.
Progression:
Without intervention: BP drops to 70/40, HR 160 then bradycardic, patient becomes unresponsive.
With needle decompression (ALS): SpO₂ rises to 92%, HR slows to 124, BP improves to 96/64. Patient remains anxious but more coherent, chest rise equalizes slightly.

Scenario #9 – Cardiac Arrest in Pregnant Patient (Recert)
30-year-old female, 34 weeks pregnant
Initial Findings: Unresponsive, no pulse, no respirations. Agonal gasps noted initially. Bystanders attempted compressions.
Progression:
With CPR + left uterine displacement: ETCO₂ 14 → 22 after compressions, HR regained briefly after 2 shocks but pulseless again.
Without uterine tilt: Poor perfusion, ineffective compressions, no ROSC after 20 min.

Scenario #10 – Hazardous Material Exposure (Recert)
40-year-old male, collapsed outside warehouse
Initial Findings: Unresponsive, pinpoint pupils, excessive salivation, muscle twitching. HR 50, BP 88/60, RR 6, SpO₂ 78%. Strong chemical odor present.
Progression:
Without antidote/airway: Respirations stop, HR slows to 30, cardiac arrest in minutes.
With BVM + atropine (ALS antidote): RR assisted to 12, SpO₂ rises to 92%, HR improves to 72, secretions dry, patient remains unconscious but stable for transport.
